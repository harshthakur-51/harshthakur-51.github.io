Low-Noise Amplifier Design using NMOS CVF OTA for Neural Recording

A UMC 180 nm CMOS neural recording front end using an NMOS Conventional Voltage Follower OTA inside a capacitive-feedback amplifier to target low input-referred noise, low power, and neural-band bandwidth for implantable BMI applications.

Overview

The work designs and simulates a low-noise neural recording amplifier for implantable Brain-Machine Interface front ends. The proposed design uses an NMOS CVF OTA as the core gain stage in a capacitive-feedback amplifier, implemented in UMC 180 nm CMOS.

Research Motivation

Neural spikes and local field potentials are small bioelectric signals, so the first analog front end must provide low noise, low power, adequate gain, and sufficient bandwidth. The thesis frames the noise-power trade-off as the central challenge for implantable neural recording circuits.

Problem Statement

The thesis targets a low-noise neural recording amplifier using an NMOS CVF OTA topology in UMC 180 nm CMOS. The goal is to maintain neural-band bandwidth and practical gain while reducing input-referred noise and power consumption under a 1 V supply.

Research Objectives

The objectives include reviewing neural amplifier and OTA literature, designing the NMOS CVF OTA, implementing a capacitive-feedback neural amplifier with 10 pF input capacitance and 200 fF feedback capacitance, suppressing DC offset with MOS pseudo-resistors, and verifying performance through AC, noise, and transient simulation.

System Architecture

The complete amplifier uses capacitive input coupling to block electrode DC offset, an NMOS CVF OTA for transconductance and gain, a 200 fF feedback capacitor to set closed-loop gain, and MOS bipolar pseudo-resistors to create the high-impedance low-frequency feedback path.

Design Methodology

The design flow moves from literature review and topology selection to Cadence Virtuoso schematic implementation, iterative transistor and passive sizing, and Cadence Spectre verification through AC analysis, noise analysis, and transient power analysis.

Circuit Architecture

The thesis identifies M7-M8 as the differential input pair, M2-M3 and M4-M5 as cross-coupled active loads, M9 as the tail current source, M6 as an active-load transistor, and M10-M11 as the output source follower stage. A base capacitor is included to suppress noise coupling and stabilize bias current.

Simulation Environment

All schematic capture and simulations are reported in Cadence Virtuoso using the Spectre simulator and UMC 180 nm CMOS models. The thesis also notes Cadence IC 6.1.8 on a Linux CentOS workstation.

Key Design Decisions

The NMOS CVF OTA was chosen over folded-cascode OTA structures for the reported noise-power advantage of the cross-coupled load. The 10 pF / 200 fF capacitor ratio sets the approximately 33 dB mid-band gain, while MOS pseudo-resistors provide DC offset suppression without large physical resistors.

Performance Summary

The thesis reports approximately 33 dB mid-band gain, approximately 13 kHz -3 dB bandwidth, approximately 0.21 uV integrated input-referred noise, approximately 1.84 uW power at 1 V, and approximately 60 degrees phase margin.

Engineering Contributions

The thesis documents Harsh Thakur's contribution as literature survey, pseudo-resistor design and characterization, and gain/bandwidth verification. The broader team contribution covers OTA architecture, simulation, transistor sizing, tabulation, documentation, and supervision.

Future Work

Future work includes physical layout, DRC/LVS, parasitic extraction, post-layout simulation, MPW fabrication, silicon measurement, Monte Carlo and mismatch analysis, MEA integration, SAR ADC integration, digital post-processing, and multi-channel expansion.
